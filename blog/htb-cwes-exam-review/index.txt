13:T1153,<p>Hack The Box renamed the CBBH certification to <strong>CWES</strong> to better reflect the broader web-application penetration testing skills employers are looking for today, rather than focusing only on traditional bug-bounty hunter roles. <sup><a href="#user-content-fn-1" id="user-content-fnref-1" data-footnote-ref aria-describedby="footnote-label">1</a></sup></p>
<p>I completed the <strong>Web Penetration Tester</strong> learning path earlier this year, and I bought my exam voucher in September last year. Life and work got hectic, and motivation was low, so it took me longer than expected to actually take the test.</p>
<h1>CWES Course Material</h1>
<p>The Web Penetration Tester path demonstrates a wide range of web attacks that are useful for the exam. Make sure you’re familiar with exploitation techniques such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)</li>
<li>SQL Injection</li>
<li>Command Injection</li>
<li>File upload vulnerabilities</li>
<li>Server-side request forgery and server-side attacks</li>
<li>Login brute-forcing and broken authentication</li>
</ul>
<p>The course is excellent for hands-on practice; however, use it as a foundation to build your own web pentest methodology. Having a consistent approach will make your exam work faster.</p>
<h1>CWES Exam Experience</h1>
<p>Disclaimer: I have a solid background in web penetration testing through bug bounty hunting and professional pentests, and I have built my own web testing methodology.</p>
<p>I took the CWES exam at the end of August, just before Hack The Box added the <em>Attacking GraphQL and API Attacks</em> module to the course. The exam itself was heavily focused on real-world web application exploitation rather than theory-based questions.</p>
<p>I used <a href="https://sysreptor.com/"><strong>SysReptor</strong></a> for my final report — it helped me structure findings and made the reporting process much smoother.</p>
<h1>Tips</h1>
<p>Here are some practical tips that helped me and will probably help you prepare for and perform well on exam day:</p>
<ol>
<li>Complete the Web Penetration Tester path, but don’t stop there. Practice on PortSwigger Labs <sup><a href="#user-content-fn-2" id="user-content-fnref-2" data-footnote-ref aria-describedby="footnote-label">2</a></sup> and other hands-on platforms.</li>
<li>Recon. Recon. Recon. Look at request/response, JavaScript, hidden endpoints, and the source code.</li>
<li>Don’t over-rely on automated tools. Always verify and understand the issue manually.</li>
<li>Build and follow your own testing methodology, covering discovery, exploitation, and reporting. Having a structured approach will help you stay organized and avoid overlooking important steps during the exam.</li>
<li>Document everything as you go. Screenshots, request/response snippets, and POC steps will save you time when writing the final report.</li>
<li>Practice reporting: being able to explain impact, exploitation steps, and remediation clearly is as important as finding the bug.</li>
</ol>
<h1>Conclusion</h1>
<p>If you already have experience with web application testing and have completed the course path, you're in a strong position to take the CWES. Focus on developing your practical exploitation skills, refining your reconnaissance and testing methodology, and practicing how to write clear, professional findings. The exam places a strong emphasis on hands-on technical skills and reporting, so be well prepared for both.</p>
